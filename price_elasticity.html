Dynamic Pricing

Introduction

In this project the client was a retailer with a huge catalogue of products. There was a need to increase prices to improve margins, but after increasing prices on some products, the client experienced a huge drop in order volumes. Concerned about this, the company was interested to know if they could use their data to avoid this situation happening again.

Objectives

The main objective of the project was to identify products with a low price elasticity, so that the client can increase product prices without seeing significant decreases order volumes. With this in mind, the main statistics we want to track are the margin and order volume. Measuring success was somewhat challenging in this project because the client was a large retailer and was unable to run a/b tests on pricing. As a result, any measurements of order volumes are susceptible to the effects of seasonality. This can be mitigated if the business has forecasts, as we can try to remove seasonality effects if we know what they are. To accurately measure success, we needed a KPI that took into account order volume and price changes. It therefore made sense to measure:

  • Estimated Volume Accuracy - Using the price elasticity scores predict what we expect the order volume to be after making a price change. After calculating this, we can compare the estimated volume with the real volume, whilst trying to account for external factors such as seasonality as best we can.

  • Total Margin (Corrected for seasonality) - The aim of the project is to increase marign, so whilst trying to eliminate external factors such as seasonality, we can compare total margin in a period before the price change with a period of equal length after the price change has occurred. This will be particularly difficult to measure for any highly seasonal products if the price change is made during a change of season, so the ideal time to make a price change is different for each product.

Theory

In pricing terms, we would say that such products have a high price elasticity (also referred to as price sensitivity). Price elasticity is defined as the change in order volumes for a given change in price. The score itself tells you how much volume is expected to change (measured in percentages) for a 1% increase in price. Traditionally, one score is calculated per product, but with advancements in machine learning it is also possible to estimate this on a case-by-case basis. For more information on this, you can check out my section on Dynamic Pricing in the Projects section of the website.

A product with high price elasticity will experience large changes in order volumes, even for small perturbations in price. There can be many factors that contribute to this, including the product category, competitor pricing, demand for the product (which may change over time), supply (within the company and competitors), quality tier (whether the product is seem as cheap, mid range or high end) and more.

Usually, many of these factors are at play at the same time, making it difficult to discern the importance of each factor to a price elasticity. Thankfully, with enough data and machine learning expertise, it is often possible to determine which products are the most/least price elastic and why. The extent to which we can explain the 'why' depends on the amount of data available, and the quality.

Methodology

In this particular project, there were years of good quality data available on most products, but minimal price changes had taken place (promotional discounts being the main exception). Since we want to estimate how volume will change with respect to price, having sufficient examples of what happens when the price changes is vital, so obtaining this data was the first step in the project.

If there are similar products with different prices, we can look at the variation in order volumes between the products to get some idea. This is not ideal because factors such as brand recognition can be responsible for large differences in sales between products of the same price. In certain markets, the more expensive item might have much higher sales (think of Heinz beans if you are in the UK).

Another solution is to run a series of tests, where the prices on products are allowed to vary randomly within a predetermined range, over different time periods. This solution will give much better results because comparing price changes on the same product mitigate the effect of brand recognition. The main issue with this solution is that such tests are very expensive to run, especially for a company that has 1000s of products.

The compromise we decided to go for was to do run pricing tests for a small number of products in different categories, and make the assumption that similar products have a similar price elasticity. For example, if the company sold a range of drilling tools, we would test one tool and use the determined price elasticity score for all other drilling tools. After doing this, price elasticity scores could be determined for all products, and aggregated by product category.
